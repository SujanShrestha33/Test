Drama And Puppetry

Early childhood education programmes that encourage exploration, creativity, and self-expression for young children include drama and puppetry. Puppetry and drama both entail the use of puppets for role-playing and storytelling, but drama incorporates the use of body language, gestures, facial emotions, and voice. Children can enhance their creative, communication, cognitive, and socioemotional abilities through both puppetry and drama (Dunn, 2003).

Importance

• According to Mayesky (2015, Chapter 15), puppetry and drama are significant subjects to teach in early childhood education because they encourage young children's creativity.
• They allow children to express themselves, learn how to communicate, develop their socioemotional skills, and play imaginatively.
• Puppetry and drama foster creativity by fostering critical thinking, problem-solving, and self-expression abilities (Mayesky, 2015).
• Puppets can boost creativity by giving children a concrete and entertaining way to express themselves and play imaginatively Anders (2021).

Resources

For young children to have a meaningful and joyful learning experience, early childhood instructors must choose tools, resources, and digital technologies that are developmentally appropriate, secure, and interesting. They also must offer direction and supervision while the children are using these tools.
• Books, props, and music/sound effects are some examples of resources for drama and puppetry in early childhood education.
• Puppets, painting and craft items, and scenery/props can all be used as materials.
• Digital cameras, cell phones, interactive whiteboards, smart boards, and internet resources, websites, and applications can all be used.
• Children may be encouraged to express themselves creatively, tell stories, and play imaginatively by using these tools, materials, and technology. This will improve their creativity and learning experiences in the subject areas of drama and puppetry.

Learning Experiences

0 to 2 years:
1. Sensory Puppet Play: For infants to explore through touch and other senses, soft, vibrant, and textured puppets can be provided.
2. Mirror Play: parents may help children study their own facial emotions and movements while using puppets to mimic them by placing a mirror in front of them.
2-3 years:
1. Puppet Storytelling: Using an easy hand or finger puppets, teachers may read aloud to young children or engage them in participatory storytelling.
2. Role play: encouraging young children to use puppets to act out known jobs such as physicians, firefighters, or cooks in dress-up play.
3 to 5 years:
1. Puppet Shows: Encouraging children to make their own puppet shows with a range of puppets and props. Teachers may help students develop, prepare, and perform their own puppet performances, encouraging creativity, storytelling, and communication abilities.
2. Puppetry and Music: Using puppets to dance or sing along to songs is an example of integrating music and movement with puppetry.
From 6 to 8 years:
1. Puppetry and Script Writing: Children can develop their storytelling, writing, and critical thinking abilities by working together to create puppet show scripts.
2. Technology and puppetry: Teaching children how to use digital tools like virtual puppets and stop-motion animation. Children may have the chance to explore the relationship between technology and creativity and improve their digital literacy through this.

Pedagogies

1. Play-based learning: Creating a play-based setting where children may play imaginatively and make up their own stories utilising drama and puppets ("Belonging, Being & Becoming," 2010).
2. Emergent curriculum: Creating drama and puppetry activities based on children's interests, concepts, and experiences while promoting engagement and self-expression ("Belonging, Being & Becoming," 2010).
3. Collaboration and social interaction: To promote cooperation, creativity, collaborative and social interactions should be facilitated throughout puppetry and theatre events.
4. Reflection and self-assessment: Encourage children to reflect on their performances, discuss concepts, and evaluate their own work (Lucas, Clinton, & Claxton, 2013).
5. Cultural inclusion and diversity: Integrating cultural inclusivity and diversity into puppetry and theatre activities to foster tolerance, acceptance, and a deeper awareness of the world around them.
