Art

The term "arts" refers to incorporating several artistic expressions within the early childhood education curriculum, including visual arts, music, dance, theatre, and literature. Art plays a crucial part in early childhood development, boosting creativity, imagination, and self-expression (Early Childhood Australia, 2021). They also help young children develop their critical thinking abilities.

Importance

• Art encourages sensory exploration and aids in the growth of children's hand-eye coordination, fine motor abilities, and spatial awareness (Gandini, 1998) 2.
• In young children, art fosters creativity, imagination, and self-expression (Isbell & Akiko-Yoshizawa, 2020).
• Young children can develop their creativity and critical thinking skills through creative activities and curricula in the visual arts, music, dance, theatre, and literature (Mayesky, 2015).
• The philosophy, theory, and pedagogy of early childhood can provide inspiration for effective art instruction (Boyd & Cutch, 2015).
• Children can be encouraged to express their ideas and participate in artistic processes by using collaboration as paedagogy, practice, and learning via art (Cutcher & Boyd, 2016).

Resources

1. crayons, markers, coloured pencils, paint, brushes, paper, glue, scissors, and various types of clay or dough.
2. Books and literature related to art, artists, and art techniques that are age-appropriate for young children.
3. Art museums and galleries, local art studios, and community art centres

Materials

1. A variety of different types of paper, including drawing paper, construction paper.
2. Recyclable materials such as cardboard, egg cartons, plastic bottles, and scrap fabrics that can be used for collage, assemblage, or other mixed media art projects.
3. Natural materials such as leaves, flowers, sticks, shells, and stones that can be used for nature-inspired art or sensory art experiences.

Digital Technologies

1. Digital drawing or painting apps and software that provide opportunities for children to experiment with digital art tools, colours, and effects.
2. Interactive art websites or apps that offer virtual art experiences, art games, or art tutorials that engage children in creating digital art in a fun and interactive way.
3. Online resources, websites, or social media platforms that provide access to art collections, virtual art exhibits, or art education materials that can be used for research or inspiration.

Learning Experiences

0 to 2 years:
• sensory exploration with secure and age-appropriate art supplies
• Handprint or footprint art using non-toxic paint
2-3 years:
• Sculpting using clay or playdough and a range of tools
• Collage art using an array of supplies and glue sticks
3 to 5 years:
• Process-oriented painting using a range of hues and brushes
• Nature-inspired art relying on gathered natural elements for collages or prints
From 6 to 8 years:
• Techniques for printing like stamping, stencilling, or mono-printing
• explore and experiment with digital art tools like drawing apps or software to develop their digital art skills and creativity.

Pedagogies

• Play-based learning: Encourage unstructured, child-led play with art supplies to encourage discovery, experimentation, and self-expression (Department of Education, Employment and Workplace Relations [DEEWR], 2009).
• Process-oriented approach: Focus on the process of creating art, emphasising experimentation, expression, and uniqueness rather than rigid obedience to norms or restrictions.
• Inquiry-based learning: Encourage students to study art themes and methods through hands-on activities by using inquiry-based learning (Arthur, 2017).
• Flexible and inclusive approaches: Recognise and respect children's different origins, areas of interest, and skills while using inclusive and adaptable techniques.
• Authentic assessment: Assess children's artistic development and creative processes using realistic assessment techniques, such as observation, recording, and reflection (White, 2016).
