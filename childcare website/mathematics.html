Mathematics and Numeracy

Young children's creativity is greatly influenced by math and numeracy. Children's creativity may be improved by a variety of arithmetic and numeracy-related activities, such as investigating patterns, forms, and sizes, as noted by Mayesky (2015). Young children can benefit from math exercises by sharpening their problem-solving, critical thinking, and reasoning skills—all of which are crucial elements of creativity.

Importance

• Children are given the opportunity to investigate, solve problems, and think critically through math and numeracy, which fosters the growth of their imagination, creativity, and ingenuity (Australian Government Department of Education, Skills and Employment, 2020).
• Early exposure to mathematics may create a strong foundation for future academic achievement and a good attitude towards STEM disciplines (Australian Curriculum, Assessment and Reporting Authority, 2021).
• Literacy and spatial awareness, which are both vital elements of creativity, can only be developed with the help of numeracy abilities (Early Childhood Australia, 2019).
• Children may learn logical thinking techniques, recognise patterns and correlations, and link disparate ideas through engaging in mathematical activities (Victorian Curriculum and Assessment Authority, 2021).
• Essential abilities that are necessary for problem-solving in any sector are fostered through mathematics (Mayesky, 2015).
• Children's overall growth can be aided by early exposure to arithmetic principles and favourable attitudes towards math.

Resources

• Counting bears, pattern blocks, and dice are a few examples of manipulatives and games that may help children learn arithmetic principles through practical applications.
• Math topics may be included in books and stories to make learning more accessible and entertaining.
• Math exercises for children may be found in digital tools and applications like Counting Caterpillar and Todo Math that are fun and interactive.
• Children can playfully explore arithmetic topics through math centres and activities (such as sorting, making patterns, and measuring).
• Math may be made more fun for children by including music and movement.
• Children can learn more and have a reference point because of visual aids and posters (such as number lines and geometric forms).
• Children can learn arithmetic naturally by engaging in outdoor exploration (e.g., counting and classifying natural items, and measuring distances).
These are just a handful of the numerous tools, resources, and digital tools early childhood educators may utilise to get students interested in the arithmetic and numeracy curriculum. It's crucial to pick games, books, and other resources that will allow children to explore and learn through play while still being developmentally appropriate.

Learning Experiences

Here are a few illustrations of learning activities for various age groups that early childhood educators might employ to foster young children's creativity in the curriculum topic of mathematics and numeracy:
0-2 years:
• sensory experiences with different shapes and textures
• exploring and counting objects.
• basic hand motions like clapping or pointing can be used to count.
2-3 years:
• Sorting and categorising items based on their colour, size, or form
• Comparing items to numbers by counting them and matching them, like in the case of counting five bricks and matching them to the number five.
• using materials or things to make basic patterns, such as alternating colours or shapes.
3-5 years:
• Building constructions out of blocks while exploring measuring and balancing principles
• introducing simple addition and subtraction through entertaining and engaging activities, such as "adding" and "subtracting" things from a fictitious grocery list
6-8 years:
• Hands-on exercises and games may be used to introduce more difficult arithmetic ideas, such as multiplication and division.
• using various materials to create and construct in order to explore geometric principles, such as making 3D forms out of marshmallows and toothpicks.
• Preparing and measuring items for a cookery project or computing distances for a scavenger hunt are a few examples of practical math problem-solving exercises.

Pedagogies

• Play-based learning: Since children learn best through play and exploration, early childhood educators should include play-based learning activities that let children explore numerical concepts fun and actively (Early Childhood Australia, 2019).
• Inquiry-based learning: Encourage students to ask questions and investigate math topics through inquiry-based learning activities, such as open-ended problem-solving and examination of actual arithmetic issues (Victorian Curriculum and Assessment Authority, 2021).
• Multisensory activities: Include various multisensory activities to help children connect with arithmetic topics in many ways, such as utilising manipulatives, music, movement, and visual aids.
• Collaborative learning: Encourage group problem-solving sessions or cooperative math projects to encourage creativity and improve social and communication skills through collaborative learning.
• Reflection and self-assessment: Encourage children to reflect on their learning and evaluate their math abilities by utilising techniques like diaries or self-evaluation checklists. This will help them take responsibility for their education and foster the growth of critical thinking abilities.
• Integration with other disciplines: Integrate math principles with other subjects like art, music, or science, to provide students with the opportunity to study math topics in a variety of situations according to Victorian Curriculum and Assessment Authority (2021).
Early childhood teachers may establish a learning environment that encourages creativity and discovery in the curriculum area of mathematics and numeracy by putting these pedagogical practises and teaching techniques into practice.
