Technologies

The inclusion of technology in the curriculum can foster young children's creativity by providing them with possibilities for learning, discovery, and expression. The National Association for the Education of Young Children and The Fred Rogers Center for Early and Children's Media both advocate for implementing interactive media and technology in the classroom. Technology makes knowledge and resources more accessible, encouraging critical thinking, problem-solving, and teamwork abilities. Using technology, educators may promote an environment where invention and creativity are valued, engaging students in fresh ways and helping them acquire skills for the future (Wilson, n.d).

Importance

Technology has a big part to play when it comes to encouraging young children's creativity.
• With the help of technology, children may experiment, explore, and create in many different ways.
• It may be used as a tool to improve conventional creative pursuits while also presenting fresh chances for artistic expression (Department of Education and Training, 2016).
• According to Howell (2012), technology may encourage creativity by giving children new means of communication and self-expression.
• The ability to cooperate and share ideas with others might encourage young children to approach creativity in a more social and cooperative way.
• Access to fresh and interesting sources of inspiration is something that technology may offer children (Australian Curriculum, Assessment and Reporting Authority, 2017).
• A curriculum that honours creativity and promotes experimentation and risk-taking must use technology.
• Giving children the chance to utilise technology in a way that is personally meaningful and related to their interests is also crucial (Australian Government Department of Education, Skills and Employment, 2021).

Resources

Early childhood educators can use the following tools, materials, and digital technology to encourage engineering among children:
• Tablets and Computers: Tablets and computers may be used to access educational websites, interactive apps, and software applications designed to help with children's literacy, numeracy, and digital skill development.
• Children may explore with simple machines, gears, motors, and sensors using robotics kits made specifically for young children.
• Online resources and games: Young children may learn engineering ideas through a variety of online resources and games. Young children may engage in programming exercises on websites like Tynker and ScratchJr.
• Digital cameras and video cameras: These tools provide children a means to express themselves, keep a record of their experiences, and grow as artists.
• Coding tools: Using enjoyable and engaging coding programmes like Scratch Jr., Scratch, and Code.org, children may learn the fundamentals of programming.

Learning Experiences

The following learning experiences for various age groups can be used by early childhood educators to foster young children's creativity in the subject of technology: 0-2 years:
• Give children basic pull-string or push-button toys that light up, play music, or move as cause-and-effect toys.
• To excite their senses, give children a range of sensory items, such as books they can touch and feel or toys that produce different noises.
2-3 years:
• Give children access to low-tech electronic gadgets with basic games and activities, including tablets or interactive whiteboards.
• Encourage children to use touchscreen devices or digital cameras to make basic digital art.
3-5 years:
• Using block-based coding applications like Scratch Jr. or Code.org, introduce children to the fundamentals of programming.
• Encourage children to use multimedia programmes like iMovie or Animoto to make their own digital stories or animations.
6-8 years:
• Utilise visual programming languages like Scratch or Blockly to teach children the fundamentals of programming.
• Encourage children to use web development tools like Wix or WordPress to create their own games, applications, or websites.
Early childhood educators may encourage young children's creativity, problem-solving skills, and digital literacy through these learning opportunities. Teachers may encourage children's curiosity and foster a lifetime enthusiasm of learning about technology by giving them age-appropriate chances for discovery and play.

Pedagogies

Early childhood educators may apply the following pedagogical techniques and teaching strategies to incorporate creativity in the technology-related curricular area:
• Play-based learning: Give children the chance to experiment with and learn about technology through play. Encourage children to utilise technology to create new things by applying their imaginations and ingenuity (Australian Government Department of Education and Training, 2017).
• Project-based learning: Incorporate technology-related activities for children through project-based learning. According to the Australian Curriculum (2013), Children may, for instance, collaborate in small groups to design and construct a straightforward robot or produce an electronic narrative.
• Integrated learning: To give children engaging and real-world learning opportunities, integrate technology education into other subject areas of the curriculum, including arithmetic or reading (Australian Curriculum, Assessment and Reporting Authority, 2013)..
• Authentic assessment: Queensland Department of Education (n.d.) emphasizes the importance of encouraging students to evaluate their own work and reflect on what they have learned as part of authentic assessment in technology education.
• Flexible learning space: Establish a flexible educational setting that encourages creativity and innovation. To inspire children to experiment and create, provide a variety of technological tools and resources, including tablets, laptops, and coding kits (New South Wales Department of Education, n.d.).
• Collaboration and communication: Encouraging children to share their ideas and research with others while working together is important. This might encourage innovation and creativity in the study of technology (Australian Curriculum, 2013).
