Music and Movement

Children's physical, cognitive, social, and emotional development can benefit greatly from the inclusion of music and movement in early childhood education. Activities including music and movement can improve gross and fine motor abilities, body awareness and coordination, creativity and self-expression, collaboration and social interaction, as well as emotional control and self-management (Niland, 2015).

Importance

• Early childhood curriculum may support and increase children's creativity, encourage holistic development, and nurture their individuality by including music and movement.
• They offer chances for social interaction, emotional expression, sensory stimulation, self-expression, storytelling, teamwork, and cognitive growth (Niland, 2015).
• Children may express their emotions, awaken their senses, pique their interest, and encourage discovery via music and movement activities.
• They promote autonomy, self-expression, and uniqueness while fostering creative problem-solving and narrative abilities (Harrison & Gibbons, 2013).
• According to Niland (2015), group exercises like singing, dancing, and improvisation help students learn social skills and collaboration.
• Movement and music also improve cognitive abilities like attention, memory, and spatial awareness.

Resources

• age-appropriate musical instruments so they may experiment with various rhythms and tones.
• recorded music introduces children to various musical genres and promotes dancing and singing.
• child-friendly digital music and movement applications.
• trustworthy websites to get lesson ideas and training videos.
• credible internet resources from educational websites, music and movement associations, and early childhood professional associations, such as lesson plans, activity suggestions, and instructional videos.

Learning Experiences

0–2 years:
• Sensory Music: Soft music combined with tactile objects (scarves, ribbons) allows young children to discover the music's rhythm and melody.
• Body Movement: Encouraging infants to move freely while listening to lively music helps them develop their gross motor abilities and their capacity for artistic expression.
2-3 years:
• Dance and Freeze: an activity that combines upbeat music with a dance and freeze element to foster creativity, coordination, and listening abilities.
• musical instruments: encourage young children to experiment and develop their own rhythms, promoting creativity and self-expression.
3-5 years:
• Creative movement: providing various music genres and movement cues to express themselves, allowing for multiple interpretations.
• music and storytelling: Instrumental music combined with acting out stories using the body fosters imagination, creativity, and storytelling abilities.
6-8 years:
• Dance choreography: providing opportunities for children to make their own dance routines, promoting imagination, cooperation, and self-expression, with popular or cultural music.
• Music Composition: Basic musical principles should be introduced, and children should be encouraged to use digital tools to compose their own music, encouraging creativity, musicianship, and technological proficiency.

Pedagogies

• Make the setting for music and movement exercises fun and interesting (Australian Government Department of Education, 2009).
• Allow children to take responsibility for their learning by following their interests and ideas.
• Promote holistic development by incorporating music and movement into other curriculum subject areas.
• Respond to children's cues by offering assistance and scaffolding as necessary.
• Promote teamwork and group projects to promote social interaction and problem-solving abilities.
• In order to encourage creativity, consider and modify your teaching methods.
• Include various musical and dance activities to encourage inclusion and respect for diversity (Early Childhood Australia, 2019).
• Place an emphasis on the music- and movement-making process while appreciating the contributions of the children (Connell & McCarthy, 2011).
